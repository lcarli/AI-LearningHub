OutdoorGear Inc. - Frequently Asked Questions

FOOTWEAR & ACCESSORIES
=======================

Q: What type of hiking boots should I buy?
A: Choose based on terrain and load. Trail running shoes / low-cut hiking shoes: best for 
well-maintained trails, light loads (under 10 kg), warm weather. More agile and faster-drying.
Mid-cut hiking boots: most versatile. Ankle support without full rigidity. Good for off-trail, 
moderate loads, varied terrain. High-cut mountaineering boots: for scrambling, snow, heavy 
loads (15+ kg), multi-day backpacking with heavy pack. More waterproof and durable.

Q: Should my hiking footwear be waterproof?
A: Waterproof boots (Gore-Tex membrane) are great for wet conditions, stream crossings, 
and cold weather — feet stay dry longer. Trade-off: waterproof membranes breathe less — 
feet get sweaty on warm days, leading to blisters. In dry conditions, non-waterproof boots 
are often preferred for breathability. A practical approach: waterproof boots for spring/fall, 
non-waterproof mesh boots for summer.

Q: How do I break in new hiking boots?
A: Wear them around the house for a few hours. Then short walks (1-2 km). Then day hikes. 
Finally, multi-day trips. Never wear new boots on a long trail without breaking them in — 
you will get severe blisters. The break-in period is typically 2-4 weeks of occasional use.
Hiking socks matter: wear merino wool or synthetic hiking socks (not cotton), with proper 
cushioning. Using liner socks reduces friction.

Q: What trekking pole features are most important?
A: Key features: adjustable length (fits different terrain: shorter uphill, longer downhill), 
cork or foam grip (reduces vibration, comfortable in cold/wet), anti-shock system (reduces 
joint impact on downhill), weight (ultralight carbon fiber vs. affordable aluminum), and 
basket type (small baskets for summer, large snow baskets for winter/mud).

Q: Do I need trekking poles?
A: Research shows trekking poles reduce knee stress by up to 25% on downhill sections — 
significant on long descents. Benefits: knee protection, better balance on uneven terrain, 
help when crossing streams, useful as tent poles for ultralight shelters. Most hikers over 
40, those with knee problems, or anyone doing long descents benefit greatly. For flat trail 
running, poles add unnecessary weight and restrict arm swing.
